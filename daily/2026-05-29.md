# Daily Note: 2026-05-29

## Topic

Women builders, network-driven distribution, and AI x Web3 career paths.

## Summary

Today's session on Women Builders in AI x Web3 was less about a specific protocol or technical stack, and more about career paths, community networks, and how early builders create opportunity in a global ecosystem.

The most useful line for me was: network is distribution. For early builders, learning technical concepts is only the first step. What actually helps a project move forward is repeated contribution, visibility, trust, and access to the right collaboration networks.

The speaker's path was practical: learn first, then contribute as early as possible. Open-source work, hackathons, ambassador programs, local meetups, X content, and community participation can all become proof of work. In Web3, GitHub, X, public contributions, and community reputation are not just branding channels. They function almost like a public credibility layer.

## Key Ideas

- Learning alone is not enough. Builders need to contribute, show work, and build trust.
- Network is not just social capital. It can become distribution for projects, ideas, jobs, and collaborators.
- English and public communication matter because much of the Web3 ecosystem is globally coordinated.
- Open-source contribution, hackathons, and ambassador programs can create entry points for people without a traditional technical or Silicon Valley background.
- AI x Web3 is powerful because AI accelerates building, while Web3 provides open, composable, and persistent infrastructure.

## My Takeaway

One Q&A question was especially relevant: if someone were starting today, should they choose AI, Web3, or AI x Web3? The answer was AI x Web3.

The reasoning was straightforward. AI gives builders leverage, but it often depends on closed platforms and private infrastructure. Web3 offers a more open environment where people can build projects, coordinate value, and create records that do not depend entirely on one company. When the two are combined, agents may become active users of Web3 infrastructure, not just tools used by humans.

This connects directly with my recent thinking around Agentic Commerce. If agents start calling tools, paying for services, interacting with wallets, or participating in on-chain systems, then the question is not only how to build faster. The question becomes how to design trust, permission, reputation, and compliance around machine actors.

## Programmable Compliance Connection

For my Programmable Compliance direction, this session reframed community and proof of work as part of the infrastructure problem.

Compliance is not only a legal checklist. In open ecosystems, trust is built through visible behavior: public contributions, audit trails, transaction records, GitHub history, governance participation, and reputation signals. These are not perfect, but they are more verifiable than private claims of good intention.

The same logic applies to AI agents. If an agent wants to act in a Web3 environment, it may need a public or semi-public trust layer: what it is allowed to do, who authorized it, what it has done before, what limits apply, and when human review is required.
